In 2018, I sat in a meeting with a large retailer reviewing their customer data infrastructure. They had years of transaction history. Loyalty program data on millions of customers. Email engagement rates, web analytics, social listening. A data warehouse that cost more per year than most small businesses make in revenue.

"What do you do with all of it?" I asked.

"Mostly? Quarterly reports for the board."

This is not unusual. In fact, it's the norm.

## The Collection Illusion

There's a comforting feeling that comes from data collection. You have evidence. You have proof. You're a "data-driven" company. The dashboards look impressive. The data science team is busy. Everything appears to be working.

But data doesn't drive decisions. *People* drive decisions, and they use data selectively — usually to confirm what they already believe, defend what they've already built, or deflect responsibility for what they're about to do.

The gap between data collected and data actually used for meaningful decisions is vast at most companies. And the larger the company, the wider the gap tends to be.

## Why Activation Fails

The failure isn't technical. It's rarely about data quality, though that's a convenient excuse. It's about three things.

**Speed.** By the time insights travel from analyst to decision-maker, the moment has passed. A customer who was at risk of churning last month cannot be saved by a campaign you launch today. The insight needs to be closer to the action — ideally embedded in the same system where the action happens.

**Trust.** Operational teams don't always trust the numbers. They've been burned before — by analyses that didn't account for something obvious, by recommendations that didn't survive contact with reality. So they ignore the data and rely on intuition anyway. Building trust is slow, deliberate work.

**Friction.** Acting on data requires changing behavior. Someone has to do something differently, which means disrupting existing processes, convincing skeptics, and accepting responsibility if it doesn't work. It's much easier to nod at the dashboard and do what you were going to do anyway.

## The Activation Model

What separates companies that actually use their data? A few patterns I've noticed consistently.

The best ones embed insights in the workflow, not alongside it. Instead of a report that someone has to read and interpret, the system presents a specific person with a specific recommendation and makes acting on it one step. The decision is made easy, not just available.

They set a very small number of leading indicators and obsess over them. Not fifty metrics. Three or four that actually correlate with the outcomes they care about. Everything else is noise — interesting, perhaps, but not acted on.

And they build feedback loops. If an action was taken based on a data-driven recommendation, the system tracks whether it worked. This builds trust in the data over time — which is the real compounding asset. Once operational teams trust a signal, they use it. Once they use it, the data gets better. The loop reinforces itself.

---

Data is not a strategy. Data *activation* is.

The retailers I work with who win at this aren't the ones with the most data. They're the ones who've figured out how to turn a specific data point into a specific action by a specific person at the right moment.

That's a product problem, not an analytics problem. And it's one worth solving.
