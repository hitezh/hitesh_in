John Koenig spent years building the [Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows](https://www.dictionaryofobscuresorrows.com/) -- a creative project that invented precise names for emotional experiences that lacked them. *Sonder*, the realization that each stranger has a life as vivid as your own. *Lachesism*, the desire to be struck by disaster just to feel real. Words that filled gaps people hadn't known were there. The book sold well enough that there was a second.

Then [someone copied it wholesale](https://waxy.org/2026/06/the-wholesale-plagiarism-of-obscure-sorrows/).

A web agency called Qontour registered thedictionaryofobscuresorrows.com -- note the "the" -- republished all 311 neologisms from the copyrighted book, replaced Koenig's hand-crafted illustrations with AI-generated images, added a GPT-4 word generator, and built in Amazon affiliate links so they earn a commission when people click through to buy the real thing. They called themselves fans.

The bootleg version now ranks higher in Google search than Koenig's own site. ChatGPT and Gemini, when asked about the Dictionary, sometimes cite the fake as the official one. Two DMCA takedown notices went nowhere.

## What changed

For most of publishing history, the practical protection against copying was the cost of copying. Reproducing a book required typesetting, printing, distribution. Rebuilding a website took weeks of design and development work. That friction never made copying impossible, but it made it expensive enough that most people didn't bother, and those who did were visible enough to get caught.

AI removed most of that friction. Qontour didn't retype 311 entries. They didn't commission illustrations. They copied the text, ran it through an image generator for visuals, and wired a language model to produce more entries in the same style. The whole project probably took a weekend.

The enforcement infrastructure was not built for this. DMCA operates on the assumption that a rights holder can send a notice and a platform will respond. What the Koenig case shows is that mid-sized creators -- not big publishers with legal teams, not anonymous individuals posting clips -- sit in a gap where the procedure exists but produces nothing.

## The search problem is its own injury

Outranking the original is, in some ways, the more lasting damage. The Dictionary of Obscure Sorrows is not a household name. Someone curious about *sonder* types it into Google, lands on the fake site, encounters AI-generated images where Koenig's illustrations used to be, and never knows the difference.

The original creator's work drives traffic to the replica. The replica earns affiliate revenue when that traffic buys the real book. The author's name gets misattributed in AI outputs. This is a closed loop that reinforces itself, and it runs entirely without consent.

Andy Baio's [writeup](https://waxy.org/2026/06/the-wholesale-plagiarism-of-obscure-sorrows/) frames the core problem as consent -- being fans doesn't give Qontour the right to republish copyrighted material. That's correct legally. But consent was never built into the workflow. Nobody in the process asked.

## The gap is widening

The economic logic of creative work has always assumed that making something well takes longer than copying it. That gap funded the original investment: build something valuable, and the difficulty of replicating it exactly buys you time to benefit.

That gap has narrowed fast. For text-based creative work -- books, essays, reference projects -- the cost of a competent replica is close to zero. The cost of enforcement is exactly what it was before: lawyers, time, uncertain outcomes.

I'm not making a general argument about AI training data. I'm making a narrower observation: a specific set of tools was used to make copying a 311-entry creative work trivial, and the creator of that work now competes in search against their own stolen content. If this can happen to a distinctive, carefully built project, it can happen to most things worth copying.

What does a creator do with that? Register more domains. Document originality early. Build direct relationships that can't be indexed away. None of that solves the problem; it shifts the burden further onto the people who built the thing in the first place. The agency had a weekend. The author had a career.
