What Nobody Tells You About Building B2B Software in a Niche Market

Notes from a decade of selling enterprise software to retailers: the patience tax, the compounding advantages, and why niche is a strategy.

Ten years ago, I decided to build software specifically for mid-market retail loyalty programs. Everyone I talked to had the same reaction: isn’t that too niche?

They weren’t wrong that it was niche. They were wrong that this was a problem.

The Case for Narrow

When you build for a specific market, a few things happen that don’t happen when you build for everyone.

You get very good, very fast. Customer conversations are dense with signal because the problems are specific and recurring. The person you talked to on Monday has the same underlying issue as the person on Friday — they just manifest differently. Patterns emerge quickly, and you stop reinventing the wheel with each new customer.

Your product gets better in ways that matter. When every customer shares a domain, feedback compounds. A feature you build for one retailer is immediately relevant to the next three. You’re not building bespoke solutions; you’re building a platform from accumulated domain knowledge.

Word travels. Mid-market retail is not a large world. People move between companies, talk at conferences, share vendors. One strong reference customer is worth ten cold outreach campaigns.

The Patience Tax

What nobody talks about is the patience tax.

B2B sales in a niche market is slow by definition. The buyer has limited alternatives, which makes them slower to move — they know you’ll still be there in six months. The evaluation process is thorough. Procurement is painful. Legal review takes months.

You will spend real time with prospects who never buy. You will customize proposals for deals that evaporate. You will lose to inertia more often than to competition.

The patience tax is real, and it accumulates. For the first few years of OptCulture, I often questioned whether the niche was too small, the sales cycle too long, the market too conservative.

What I didn’t see clearly until later: we were building something competitors couldn’t easily replicate. Genuine domain depth. Hard-won customer relationships. A product shaped by thousands of conversations with people who understood their own problems in granular detail.

None of that shows up on a balance sheet in year two.

The Compounding Advantage

Here’s what changes after a certain point: the advantages compound.

Your customer success is better because you’ve seen every edge case. Your product roadmap is cleaner because you know exactly what matters to your buyers and what’s noise. Your sales cycles shorten because you have reference customers in the same industry who can speak to the exact same concerns.

And your competitive position is surprisingly durable. A horizontal platform can always add your features. But they can’t replicate five years of domain knowledge, customer relationships, and edge-case handling without spending those five years. By the time they get there, you’ve spent five more.

Niche is not the same as small. It’s a strategy — one that demands patience in the early years but creates real defensibility over time.

If I were starting over, I’d still pick the niche. I’d just prepare for the patience tax a little better, and worry a little less in year two.
