Loyalty Beyond Points: Why Your Rewards Program Isn't Working

Most loyalty programs are built on the wrong foundation. Here's what actually drives customers to come back.

Every retailer I talk to has the same complaint: “We launched a points program, but customers aren’t engaged.”

The program works mechanically. Points accumulate. Emails go out. Redemptions happen. The dashboard shows activity. But the numbers that actually matter — repeat visit rate, basket size, net promoter score — barely moved.

Here’s the uncomfortable truth: a points program isn’t a loyalty program. It’s a discount program with extra steps.

What Loyalty Actually Is

Loyalty is a behavioral state where customers prefer you — not because you’re cheaper, not because they’ve accumulated sunk costs in your program, but because the experience of doing business with you is consistently better than the alternative.

That preference is built through consistent positive experiences across every touchpoint. It’s maintained through relevance — the feeling that this business understands what I need before I tell them. And it’s deepened by recognition — the sense that they know who I am.

A points program can reinforce this. It cannot create it.

The Points Trap

Here’s what most points programs actually do: they attract deal-seekers — customers who have no particular attachment to your brand and will leave the moment they find a better offer elsewhere. These are the customers who game the program, redeem everything, and disappear.

Meanwhile, your genuinely loyal customers — the ones who were already coming back — are quietly subsidizing discounts for people who never intended to stay.

You’ve built a program that’s most attractive to exactly the customers you’re least interested in retaining.

What Works Instead

The retailers I’ve seen build genuine loyalty share a few characteristics.

They use data to recognize, not just reward. When a customer walks in and is greeted by name, or gets a recommendation based on what they actually buy, that’s loyalty-building. When they receive a relevant alert about a product they’ve been looking at — that’s loyalty-building. Points for a fifth visit is not.

They design for relationships, not transactions. This sounds abstract but it’s operational. It means training staff to notice regulars. It means personalizing communication based on actual purchase behavior, not demographic segments. It means treating a complaint as an opportunity rather than a cost.

They measure the right things. Not redemption rates. Not points issued. Customer lifetime value, retention cohorts, visit frequency by segment. The program should drive these numbers; if it isn’t, something is wrong — and the right question isn’t “how do we improve redemptions?” but “what are we doing wrong with the experience?”

None of this means points programs are worthless. They can be effective tools within a broader engagement strategy. But when the program is the strategy, you’ve already ceded the most important ground.

The question isn’t “how do we make our points program better?” It’s “what experience are we creating that makes a customer want to choose us again?”

Start there. Build backwards.
